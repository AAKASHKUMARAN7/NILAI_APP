---

The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023 replaces the Indian Penal Code (IPC), 1860. It came into effect on July 1, 2024. The BNS modernizes India's criminal law framework with 358 sections (compared to IPC's 511 sections). Key changes include: new offences for terrorism, organized crime, mob lynching, and petty organized crime. It introduces community service as a punishment for minor offences. The BNS removes sedition (Section 124A IPC) and replaces it with a narrower offence against sovereignty, unity and integrity of India (Section 152 BNS). It also adds provisions for offences against women and children, and introduces specific provisions for hit-and-run cases.

---

id: bns-sec-4
category: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)
title: BNS Section 4 — Punishments
part: Chapter II - Punishments
keywords: punishment, types of punishment, fine, imprisonment, community service, death, life imprisonment, bns
---

The punishments to which offenders are liable under the provisions of this Sanhita are: (a) Death; (b) Imprisonment for life; (c) Imprisonment — Rigorous (with hard labour) or Simple; (d) Fine; (e) Community Service. Community service is a new form of punishment introduced by BNS for petty offences, meaning work assigned by the Court which a convict is required to perform without any remuneration.

---

id: bns-sec-103
category: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)
title: BNS Section 103 — Murder (replaces IPC Section 302)
part: Chapter VI - Of Offences Affecting the Human Body
keywords: murder, homicide, killing, death, life, punishment for murder, section 302, bns 103
---

Whoever commits murder shall be punished with death, or imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine. Murder by a group of five or more persons on grounds of race, caste or community, sex, place of birth, language, personal belief or any other similar ground shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life which shall mean the remainder of that person's natural life, and shall also be liable to fine. This provision specifically addresses mob lynching — a significant new addition compared to the old IPC.

---

id: bns-sec-63-70
category: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)
title: BNS Sections 63-70 — Sexual Offences Against Women
part: Chapter V - Of Offences Against Women and Children
keywords: rape, sexual assault, sexual offence, women, consent, bns, punishment for rape, marital rape, gang rape, pocso
---

BNS Section 63 defines rape similarly to the erstwhile IPC Section 375, but with enhanced punishments. Section 64 provides punishment for rape — not less than 10 years rigorous imprisonment, extendable to life. Section 65 deals with gang rape — not less than 20 years RI, extendable to life, meaning natural life. Section 66 covers rape of minor girls — death penalty or imprisonment for remainder of natural life for rape of girl under 12. Section 69 relates to sexual intercourse by employing deceitful means — a new addition. Section 70 specifically criminalizes sexual intercourse by a person in authority. The BNS does NOT criminalize marital rape where the wife is over 18 years of age.

---

id: bns-sec-152
category: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)
title: BNS Section 152 — Acts endangering sovereignty, unity and integrity of India (replaces Sedition)
part: Chapter VII - Of Offences Against the State
keywords: sedition, sovereignty, unity, integrity, anti-national, state, section 124a replacement, free speech
---

Whoever, purposely or knowingly, by words, either spoken or written, or by signs, or by visible representation, or by electronic communication or by use of financial means, or otherwise, excites or attempts to excite, secession or armed rebellion or subversive activities, or encourages feelings of separatist activities or endangers sovereignty or unity and integrity of India; or indulges in or commits any such act shall be punished with imprisonment for life or with imprisonment which may extend to seven years and shall also be liable to fine. This replaces the colonial-era sedition law (IPC Section 124A) with a narrower scope — mere criticism of the government is not an offence; the act must involve actual incitement to secession or armed rebellion.

id: bnss-overview
category: Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS)
title: Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 — Overview (replaces CrPC)
part: General
keywords: bnss, bharatiya nagarik suraksha sanhita, crpc replacement, criminal procedure, new crpc, 2023
---

The Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023 replaces the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973. It came into effect on July 1, 2024. Key changes include: mandatory videography of crime scenes for all offences punishable with 7+ years; electronic FIR registration (Zero FIR can be filed at ANY police station regardless of jurisdiction); mandatory forensic investigation for offences with 7+ years punishment; timelines for judgments (45 days from completion of arguments); timeline for charge framing (60 days from first hearing); provision for trial in absentia; electronic summons via SMS/email; police must inform the arrested person's nominated person within 24 hours; and a new provision for mercy petitions with a timeline.

---

id: bnss-arrest-rights
category: Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS)
title: BNSS — Rights During Arrest and Detention
part: Chapter V - Arrest
keywords: arrest, detention, rights, bail, handcuff, police, fir, zero fir, custody, bnss, new law
---

Under the BNSS: (1) Zero FIR — An FIR can now be filed at ANY police station, regardless of where the offence occurred (Section 173 BNSS). The FIR must be transferred to the jurisdictional police station within 15 days. (2) The arrested person must be informed of the grounds of arrest in writing. (3) A nominated person must be informed of the arrest. (4) Handcuffing is permitted only in specific serious cases like terrorism, drug offences, murder, organized crime, and offences against the State. (5) Police custody cannot exceed 15 days, but can now be requested in parts during the first 40/60 days (depending on severity). (6) Bail provisions have been reformed — undertrial prisoners who have served one-third of maximum sentence for first offence (or one-half for subsequent) are entitled to bail (Section 479 BNSS). (7) All searches must be videographed and recorded.

id: bsa-overview
category: Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA)
title: Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 — Overview (replaces Indian Evidence Act)
part: General
keywords: bsa, bharatiya sakshya adhiniyam, evidence act, new evidence law, digital evidence, electronic evidence, 2023
---

The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023 replaces the Indian Evidence Act, 1872. It came into effect on July 1, 2024. Key changes: (1) Electronic and digital records are now treated as primary evidence (not secondary), including emails, SMS, social media posts, digital photos, and server logs. (2) Electronic devices like smartphones, laptops are included in the definition of 'document'. (3) Oral evidence can be given electronically (video conferencing allowed). (4) Specific provisions for admissibility of electronic evidence with proper certificate (Section 63 BSA). (5) Joint trials provisions updated. (6) Secondary evidence provisions expanded and modernized.

title: Introduction to the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita
part: Part I
keywords: criminal law, introduction, bharatiya nyaya sanhita
---

The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) is the new criminal law code of India, introduced to modernize and streamline the criminal justice system. This law replaces the colonial-era Indian Penal Code (IPC) and aims to address the evolving nature of crime, including cybercrimes, terrorism, and white-collar crimes.

The BNS introduces new sections on the rights of the accused, strengthening victim protection, and the establishment of faster courts to deal with pending cases.

id: bnss-sec-2
category: Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita
title: New Procedures for Investigation
part: Part II
keywords: investigation, procedures, bharatiya nyaya sanhita
---

This section outlines the procedures for criminal investigations under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita. It mandates strict timeframes within which investigations should be completed and introduces provisions for forensic audits in serious cases like cybercrimes.

The law also emphasizes the use of technology in crime investigations, allowing for digital evidence to be admissible in court.

id: dpdp-sec-1
category: Digital Personal Data Protection
title: Introduction to Digital Personal Data Protection
part: Part I
keywords: data protection, digital, personal data, privacy, legislation
---

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) is India’s first comprehensive law that safeguards the privacy of individuals’ personal data. This law aims to regulate how personal data is collected, processed, and stored by companies, with provisions for penalties for non-compliance.

The DPDPA also establishes the Data Protection Authority (DPA) to ensure the enforcement of data protection standards and handle grievances.

---

id: dpdp-sec-2
category: Digital Personal Data Protection
title: Rights of Data Subjects
part: Part II
keywords: data subject, rights, data protection, privacy
---

This section grants individuals specific rights over their personal data, including the right to access, rectify, and erase their data. It ensures that data subjects are informed about how their data is used and gives them the right to withdraw consent at any time.

Companies must ensure transparency and provide clear channels for individuals to exercise these rights.
