title: Situation — Arrested by Police or Facing Police Action
part: Situations
keywords: arrested, police, detention, custody, FIR, bail, rights, beating, torture, station, lockup, complaint, handcuff
---

SITUATION: You or someone you know has been arrested, detained, or is facing police action.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty), Article 22 (Protection against arrest and detention), Article 20 (Protection against self-incrimination and double jeopardy).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Right to know the grounds of arrest — police MUST inform you in writing (Article 22 + D.K. Basu guidelines). (2) Right to be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours (Article 22). (3) Right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of your choice (Article 22). (4) Right against self-incrimination — you cannot be forced to confess (Article 20(3)). (5) Right to inform a family member or friend about your arrest. (6) Right to medical examination at the time of arrest. (7) Women cannot be arrested between sunset and sunrise except in exceptional circumstances, and must be searched only by a female officer. (8) No handcuffing except in cases of terrorism, organized crime, etc. (under BNSS). (9) Right to bail — bail is the rule, jail is the exception. For bailable offences, bail is a right. (10) Free legal aid through NALSA if you cannot afford a lawyer (Article 39A).

WHAT TO DO: File Zero FIR at any police station (BNSS Section 173). Call Legal Aid helpline 15100. If torture/beating — file complaint with NHRC and Judicial Magistrate. For illegal detention — file habeas corpus writ in High Court (Article 226).

id: situation-workplace-harassment
category: Situation Guide
title: Situation — Workplace Harassment or Sexual Harassment
part: Situations
keywords: workplace, harassment, sexual harassment, boss, employer, POSH, ICC, complaint, women, hostile, touch, inappropriate
---

SITUATION: You are facing harassment (sexual or otherwise) at your workplace.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 14 (Equality), Article 15 (Non-discrimination), Article 19(1)(g) (Right to practice profession), Article 21 (Right to dignity), Vishaka Guidelines (1997).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Right to a safe workplace free from sexual harassment (Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan, 1997). (2) Every workplace with 10+ employees must have an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) under POSH Act, 2013. (3) Right to file a complaint within 3 months of the incident (extendable by 3 months with ICC's permission). (4) Right to confidentiality during and after proceedings. (5) Right to interim relief including transfer of the respondent. (6) If there is no ICC, file with the Local Complaints Committee at the district level. (7) Protection from retaliation. (8) Right to choose formal inquiry or conciliation (except in cases involving sexual intercourse).

WHAT TO DO: Document everything. File written complaint with ICC. If no ICC exists, contact the District Women and Child Development Officer. Call Women Helpline 181. File police complaint if it involves criminal offence. Contact National Commission for Women: ncw.nic.in or 7827-170-170.

id: situation-property-eviction
category: Situation Guide
title: Situation — Property Dispute, Eviction, or Land Acquisition
part: Situations
keywords: property, eviction, land, house, flat, acquisition, builder, tenant, landlord, demolition, encroachment, compensation, RERA
---

SITUATION: You are facing eviction, property dispute, illegal land acquisition, or builder fraud.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 300A (Right to Property — no deprivation except by law), Article 21 (Right to shelter — SC interpretation), Article 14 (Equality — fair procedure).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) No property can be taken without authority of law (Article 300A). (2) Land acquisition requires: public purpose, social impact assessment, consent (80% for private/70% for PPP), and fair compensation at 2-4x market value (RFCTLARR Act, 2013). (3) Right to shelter is part of right to life (Olga Tellis v. BMC, 1985). (4) Slum dwellers cannot be evicted without notice and rehabilitation. (5) Tenant eviction requires court order — no self-help eviction by landlord. (6) Builder delays — file with RERA Authority for builder accountability. (7) Right to fair compensation for acquired property. (8) Right to challenge acquisition in court.

WHAT TO DO: For builder disputes — file with RERA (Real Estate Regulatory Authority). For land acquisition — challenge before District Judge or HC under RFCTLARR Act. For illegal eviction — file civil suit for injunction. File RTI about land records. Contact DLSA for free legal aid. For encroachment by government — file PIL or writ petition.

part: Situations
keywords: caste, discrimination, dalit, untouchability, SC, ST, atrocity, exclusion, denied entry, humiliation, atrocities act
---

SITUATION: You or someone you know is facing caste-based discrimination, untouchability, or atrocity.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 14 (Equality), Article 15 (Prohibition of discrimination), Article 17 (Abolition of Untouchability), Article 46 (Promotion of SC/ST interests).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Untouchability in ANY form is abolished and punishable (Article 17). (2) No discrimination on grounds of caste in access to public places, shops, restaurants, wells, roads (Article 15(2)). (3) Right to reservation in education and employment (Articles 15(4) and 16(4)). (4) Protection under SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 — covers humiliation, forced labour, denial of access, land dispossession, sexual exploitation, parading, social boycott. (5) FIR must be registered immediately for atrocity complaints — police cannot conduct preliminary inquiry (Subhash Kashinath Mahajan case modified by SC review). (6) Right to compensation and rehabilitation. (7) Special courts for speedy trial.

WHAT TO DO: File FIR at any police station under SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act. Contact National Commission for Scheduled Castes: ncsc.nic.in. Contact NHRC for human rights violation. File PIL for systemic issues. Call Legal Aid: 15100. File complaint with District Magistrate.

keywords: school, education, denied admission, RTE, fee, capitation, tuition, dropout, expulsion, discrimination, child, scholarship
---

SITUATION: A child is denied education, charged capitation fee, expelled unfairly, or facing educational discrimination.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 21A (Right to Free and Compulsory Education for age 6-14), Article 29 (Minority educational rights), Article 30 (Minority institutions), Article 45 (Early childhood care), Article 46 (SC/ST educational advancement).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Every child aged 6-14 has the fundamental right to free and compulsory education (Article 21A, RTE Act 2009). (2) No school can refuse admission to any child. (3) Private unaided schools must reserve 25% seats for EWS/disadvantaged children with government reimbursement (RTE Section 12(1)(c)). (4) No capitation fee can be charged (Mohini Jain v. Karnataka). (5) No child can be held back or expelled till completion of elementary education. (6) No corporal punishment allowed.  (7) Mother tongue instruction at primary level for linguistic minorities (Article 350A). (8) Pre-matric and post-matric scholarships available for SC/ST/OBC/Minority students.

WHAT TO DO: File complaint with Block Education Officer or District Education Officer. Approach State Commission for Protection of Child Rights. File with NCPCR: ncpcr.gov.in. For fee disputes — approach Fee Regulatory Committee. For capitation fee — file writ petition in High Court. Call Childline 1098.

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Protection under Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 — covers physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, and economic abuse. (4) If dowry demanded — offence under Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 and Section 498A IPC/BNS Section 86. (5) Right to maintenance under Section 125 CrPC/BNSS. (6) Right to file for divorce on grounds of cruelty. (7) Right to free legal aid.

part: Situations
keywords: cheated, consumer, product, defective, fraud, refund, online, ecommerce, delivery, warranty, quality, overcharged, complaint
---

SITUATION: You bought a defective product, received poor service, or were cheated in an online transaction.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 21 (Right to quality goods/services — SC interpretation), Article 14 (Equal treatment), Article 19(1)(g) (Right to trade — includes consumer protection).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Right to safety — products must be safe. (2) Right to information — complete details of product/service. (3) Right to choose — no forced purchases. (4) Right to be heard — your complaint must be addressed. (5) Right to redressal — compensation for defective goods/services. (6) Right to consumer education. (7) Under Consumer Protection Act, 2019: file at District Commission (claims up to ₹1 crore), State Commission (₹1-10 crore), National Commission (above ₹10 crore). (8) E-commerce platforms must appoint Grievance Officer who must respond within 1 month. (9) Product liability — manufacturer/seller/service provider liable for defective products/deficient services.

WHAT TO DO: File complaint at consumerhelpline.gov.in or call 1800-11-4000 (toll-free). File at Consumer Forum (no lawyer required). For online fraud — report at cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930. For food safety — report to FSSAI/Food Safety Officer. File with ASCI for misleading ads. Keep all bills, receipts, communications as evidence.

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Right to Privacy is a fundamental right (Puttaswamy v. Union of India, 2017). (2) Under DPDP Act, 2023 — right to access, correction, erasure of your personal data. (3) Right to informed consent before data collection. (4) Right to withdraw consent for data processing. (5) Data breach — the organization must notify you and the Data Protection Board. (6) Penalties up to ₹250 crore for failure to protect data. (7) Sharing intimate images without consent — offence under IT Act Section 66E (up to 3 years imprisonment). (8) Voyeurism — BNS Section 77 (up to 3-7 years). (9) Right to be forgotten (emerging jurisprudence).

WHAT TO DO: For cyber crime — report at cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930. File FIR under IT Act and BNS provisions. For data breaches — file complaint with the Data Protection Board of India. For Aadhaar misuse — report at uidai.gov.in. For intimate image abuse — file FIR under IT Act Section 66E and BNS Section 77. Screenshot and preserve evidence. Contact a cyber law expert through DLSA.

id: situation-employment-fired
category: Situation Guide
title: Situation — Fired from Job, Unpaid Wages, or Workplace Exploitation
part: Situations
keywords: fired, terminated, dismissed, salary, wages, unpaid, overtime, unsafe, exploitation, contract, employer, PF, ESIC, gratuity, retrenchment
---

SITUATION: You have been wrongfully terminated, denied wages, or exploited at your workplace.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 19(1)(g) (Right to practice any profession), Article 21 (Right to livelihood — Olga Tellis, 1985), Article 23 (Prohibition of forced labour), Article 39(d) (Equal pay for equal work), Article 41 (Right to work), Article 43 (Living wage).

YOUR RIGHTS: (1) Right to livelihood is a fundamental right (Olga Tellis v. BMC). (2) Wrongful termination without notice is illegal — Industrial Disputes Act requires 1-3 months notice or pay. (3) Retrenchment of workmen with 1+ year of service requires 1 month notice, retrenchment compensation (15 days' pay per year of service), and government permission for factories/establishments with 100+ workers. (4) Right to minimum wages — Payment of Wages Act. (5) Right to overtime at 2x normal rate. (6) Right to PF (12% employer + 12% employee), ESIC, gratuity (15 days' salary per year after 5 years). (7) Equal pay for equal work regardless of gender. (8) No forced labour or bonded labour (Article 23).

WHAT TO DO: File complaint with Labour Commissioner. For wage disputes — approach Labour Court. File with EPFO for PF issues: 1800-118-005. File with ESIC for health insurance. For bonded labour — contact District Magistrate for release and rehabilitation. Contact trade union for support. DLSA for free legal aid: 15100.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 19(1)(a) (Freedom of speech and expression — includes internet — Anuradha Bhasin, 2020), Article 21 (Right to access internet), Shreya Singhal judgment (2015).

(2) Section 66A IT Act (criminalizing "offensive" online content) is unconstitutional (Shreya Singhal, 2015). (5) Intermediary Guidelines (IT Rules, 2021) require platforms to have grievance redressal mechanism. (6) Cyberbullying, stalking, identity theft are criminal offences under IT Act and BNS. (7) Impersonation online is punishable under IT Act Section 66D.

WHAT TO DO: For harassment — report at cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930. File FIR at local cyber police station. For content takedown — appeal to platform's Grievance Officer, then Grievance Appellate Committee. For internet shutdown — challenge in High Court citing Anuradha Bhasin. Screenshot and preserve all evidence. For identity theft — file FIR under IT Act Section 66C. Contact cyber law experts through DLSA.

SITUATION: You want information from a government body, or the government is not being transparent.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 19(1)(a) (Right to know — SC interpretation), RTI Act, 2005.

id: situation-PIL-how-to
category: Situation Guide
title: Situation — How to File a Public Interest Litigation (PIL)
part: Situations
keywords: PIL, public interest litigation, writ, Article 32, Article 226, Supreme Court, High Court, public cause, social justice, filing
---

SITUATION: You want to file a PIL for a public cause — environmental damage, government inaction, systemic rights violation.

CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTION: Article 32 (Writ petition in Supreme Court), Article 226 (Writ petition in High Court).

YOUR RIGHTS AND PROCESS: (1) Any person can file a PIL — you don't need to be personally aggrieved. (2) Even a letter or postcard to the Chief Justice can be treated as PIL (Sunil Batra v. Delhi Administration). (3) PIL can be filed in Supreme Court (Article 32) or High Court (Article 226) for any fundamental rights violation. (4) PIL has been used for: environmental protection, prison reform, child labour elimination, sexual harassment guidelines, right to food, right to education, election reform. (5) The Court can appoint commissions, give directions to government, and monitor implementation.

HOW TO FILE: (a) Identify the public interest issue clearly. (b) Collect facts, evidence, newspaper reports, data. (c) Draft a petition stating: facts, fundamental rights violated, relief sought. (d) For Supreme Court — file through an Advocate on Record. For High Court — file through any advocate. (e) Filing fee: nominal. (f) You can send a letter/petition to the Chief Justice of HC/SC with: your name, address, facts of the case, prayer for relief. (g) Court will decide whether to entertain it. Warning: frivolous PILs attract penalty. Cost can be imposed on petitioners who file for personal gain.
